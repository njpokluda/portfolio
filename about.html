About Nick

I've always been interested in understanding why something works.

My career in marketing started with an interest in understanding how things work — not just what's happening, but why. That curiosity has shaped everything from how I approach a campaign to how I build a reporting framework.

I've spent over a decade at Austin Telco Federal Credit Union, where I've had the opportunity to grow from a hybrid marketing and IT role into leading marketing analytics and digital strategy for a $2.5 billion financial institution. That journey gave me a grounded understanding of how marketing connects to the broader business — and how data, when used well, can make that connection much stronger.

My work today focuses on turning data into clarity.

Most marketing teams have more data than they know what to do with. The challenge isn't access — it's making sense of it in a way that actually informs decisions. That's where I focus my energy: building frameworks, dashboards, and processes that help teams understand what's working, what isn't, and where to go next.

I've led projects ranging from Power BI dashboard development to customer segmentation strategy to full website redesigns. Each of these started with the same question: what problem are we actually trying to solve? The answer to that question shapes everything else.

I believe marketing works best when it balances data and intuition.

One of the challenges I've run into throughout my career is the organizational gap between data and decision-making. Teams often have access to analytics tools, but the insights those tools generate don't always make it into strategy conversations. Reports get created, reviewed, and filed away — without translating into action.

I've tried to close that gap by focusing not just on producing accurate data, but on presenting it in a way that resonates with different audiences — from marketing coordinators to executive leadership. Numbers alone rarely change minds. Context, framing, and a clear narrative around the data are what make the difference.

At the same time, I think good marketing requires more than data literacy. Intuition, empathy, and creative judgment are still essential — especially in understanding how customers think and feel. The best work I've done has come from bringing both together.

I'm naturally collaborative and focused on clarity.

I tend to sit at the intersection of marketing and other functions — whether that's IT, compliance, or executive leadership. That position has taught me how to translate between different perspectives and priorities, and how to advocate for marketing objectives in contexts where they're not always the first concern.

Clarity is something I care about deeply — in communication, in reporting, in strategy. I think a lot of organizational friction comes from ambiguity: unclear expectations, inconsistent data definitions, reports that mean different things to different people. I try to reduce that friction wherever I can, by making things simpler, more consistent, and easier to act on.

Outside of work, I like to stay curious.

I'm a big fan of escape rooms — there's something satisfying about working through a problem under pressure with a team. I have two dogs who take up a disproportionate amount of my time and energy (worth it). I play video games, build LEGO sets, and try to get out for a round of golf when the schedule allows. And I have an inexplicable fascination with iceberg videos — the ones that show just how much is hidden beneath the surface. That metaphor probably says something about how I approach my work.

Looking ahead…

I'm interested in roles where I can continue to grow as a strategic contributor, connecting marketing, data, and customer behavior to drive better outcomes. Marketing is always evolving, and I enjoy being in environments where I can keep learning, building, and pushing ideas forward.
