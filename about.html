About Nick

I've always been interested in understanding why something works.

I grew up curious about how the world works, taking apart toys and small electronics just to see how they fit together — even if I couldn't always get them back together. That same instinct shaped how I approach my work today: I want to understand the system behind the outcome, not just the surface-level result.

That curiosity led me into marketing, where the questions are rarely simple and the answers are almost always interconnected. Every marketing problem is really a question about people — what they're trying to do, what's getting in their way, and what would actually help.

My work today focuses on turning data into clarity.

Most of the problems I find interesting come down to a research question: what's actually happening, and why? I like getting close to the data, asking better questions of it, and translating what I find into something a team can act on. The most useful thing analytics can do is reduce the surface area of a decision — narrowing the space of "we could do anything" down to "here's what the evidence supports."

Data and intuition aren't opposites to me. The best marketing decisions I've been part of came from people who knew the customer and the business well, used data to pressure-test their instincts, and weren't afraid to update their thinking when the numbers pushed back. I aim to bring that same posture to my work — rigorous, but never robotic.

I believe marketing works best when it balances data and intuition.

In a lot of organizations, marketing data lives in too many places — inside platforms, in IT-owned reports, locked behind tools only a few people understand. That fragmentation makes it hard to act with confidence, and it tends to push teams toward whatever option feels safest rather than whatever option is best.

I tend to look for the middle ground: building reporting and frameworks that are robust enough to trust, but simple enough to share. If a stakeholder can't tell what they're looking at within a few seconds, the chart isn't doing its job. Clarity is a multiplier — once everyone is looking at the same picture, decisions move faster and conversations get a lot more productive.

I'm naturally collaborative and focused on clarity.

A lot of my work happens at the seams of an organization — between marketing and IT, between strategy and execution, between what leadership wants to know and what the data actually says. I enjoy being a bridge between those teams and translating between the different languages they speak.

Communication is part of the job, not a side effect of it. I try to make sure the people I work with understand the trade-offs behind a recommendation, not just the recommendation itself. When everyone has the same context, you don't need to win debates — you just have better ones.

Outside of work, I like to stay curious.

I'm a fan of escape rooms — there's something satisfying about reading a room, finding a thread, and pulling on it until something gives. I spend a lot of time with my dogs, who keep me out of my head, and I'll happily lose an evening to a good video game or to building something out of LEGO that probably has too many pieces.

I also play golf when I can — it's the closest sport I've found to a long puzzle — and I've been known to fall down YouTube rabbit holes about icebergs, deep-sea creatures, or anything that runs at a different scale than everyday life. I think a lot of those interests share the same DNA as my work: they reward patience, curiosity, and a willingness to keep looking.

Looking ahead…

I'm interested in roles where I can continue to grow as a strategic contributor, connecting marketing, data, and customer behavior to drive better outcomes. Marketing is always evolving, and I enjoy being in environments where I can keep learning, building, and pushing ideas forward.
